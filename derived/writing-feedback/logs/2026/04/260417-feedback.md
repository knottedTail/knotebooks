## Style and English Feedback

- The overall structure is good: seminar summary first, then your personal reflection, then the schedule note. That order fits a research log well.
- Several sentences become hard to read because they try to explain too much at once. This happens especially in the paragraph about removing the cone of a rigid vertex. Shorter sentences would make the logic easier to follow.
- The most frequent language issue is literal phrasing, such as "for cutting my hair," "consult a plan with GPT," and "tried to my own job." When you are unsure, it is better to choose a simpler verb phrase.
- Article usage still needs attention: phrases like "the lengths," "the link of a vertex," and "a proposition" sound more natural when the article is explicit.
- Your reflective paragraph reads more naturally when you join related ideas with a clear connector, for example "I should find a way ..., and I will work out a plan ..."

## Mathematical Writing Feedback

- The definition of a rigid vertex is stated clearly, but the surrounding explanation would benefit from tighter notation. In particular, say explicitly what "neighboring faces of $v$" means if you want to revisit this note later without the talk context.
- The sentence "There is a proposition which states that there exists a rigid vertex for any polyhedron" should be made more precise in future notes if the exact statement matters. As written, it records the existence claim, but not the assumptions under which it holds.
- The induction paragraph contains the main mathematical idea, but it mixes several levels of explanation at once: what is removed, what data are preserved, what new faces may appear, and why Steinitz's theorem is relevant. Breaking this into three or four shorter sentences would make the proof strategy much easier to recover later.
- The phrase "the remaining polyhedron is also in ED condition" is informative if you already know the talk, but it is too compressed for future reference. If possible, record what "ED condition" means or what data are preserved under the removal step.
- The final sentence about Steinitz's theorem is useful, because it marks a genuine subtlety. It would be even stronger if you said explicitly whether Steinitz's theorem is used to justify realizability, combinatorial structure, or the existence of the new polyhedron after the surgery.

I had a regular team seminar this morning.

It was about ED-rigidity for nonconvex polyhedra.
I am recording a short review of it for future reference.

Suppose that we have a 3-dimensional polyhedron in Euclidean space and we know the lengths of all the edges and all the dihedral angles.
In this situation, the question is whether the polyhedron is geometrically rigid, that is, whether every polyhedron with the same edge lengths and dihedral angles is congruent to it.
It is well known that the answer is yes for convex polyhedra, but it is not known for nonconvex ones in general.
Seonhwa Kim proved that the answer is also yes for nonconvex ones by adapting the notion of rigid vertices.

The vertex $v$ is called \emph{rigid} if $\mathrm{nt}(v) \leq 3$, where $\mathrm{nt}(v)$ is the number of triangles among the neighboring faces of $v$.
The number $3$ is related to the fact that if we know all the lengths in the link of a vertex $v$, then we know all the face angles when $\mathrm{nt}(v) \leq 3$.
There is a proposition stating that there exists a rigid vertex for any polyhedron.

He proved the theorem by using induction on the number of vertices of a polyhedron.
At a rigid vertex $v$, we can remove one vertex by cutting off the ``cone'' at $v$ from the given polyhedron.
The key point is that we know all the edge lengths and dihedral angles of the link of $v$, and the remaining polyhedron also satisfies the ED condition.
The caution is that we may add edges and faces when we remove the cone, and how they are added depends on the geometric condition of the polyhedron, not just the combinatorial one.
In other words, the statement that we still obtain a polyhedron after removing the cone is not automatic; it is a corollary of \emph{Steinitz's theorem}.

## Next-Time Checklist

- Use simple time expressions such as "this morning," "after that," and "this weekend."
- Prefer natural verb patterns like "give a talk," "get my hair cut," and "get back to work."
- When summarizing mathematics, separate the definition, the main theorem, and the proof idea into shorter sentences.
- If you use a compressed technical phrase such as "ED condition," add one short reminder of what it means.
