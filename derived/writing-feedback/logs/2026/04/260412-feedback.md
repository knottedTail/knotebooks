## Style and English Feedback

- Your writing is strongest when you use short, direct sentences such as "Maybe I need to separate folders for raw files, refined files, and reconstructed files." This sentence is clear and easy to follow.
- Several sentences sound like direct translations of thought rather than natural English prose. In particular, phrases like "configure daily routine," "perform refinement," and "AI should involve" are understandable but not idiomatic. In English, simpler verb choices usually sound better: "set up a routine," "refine the writing," and "AI should be involved."
- You often join several process steps into one long sentence. For research logs, this is a good place to use sequencing words such as "first," "then," "after that," and "finally." That will make your workflow easier to scan later.
- Your tone is appropriately reflective for a daily log. I would preserve that tone rather than forcing everything into formal paper style.
- The entry would read more smoothly if you consistently distinguish among planning, completed actions, and future tasks. Right now, those sometimes blend together.

## Mathematical Writing Feedback

- This entry is not heavily mathematical, but it is still a research log, so structural clarity matters.
- When you mention "refinement" and "reconstruction of the research result," it would help to define these stages a little more explicitly. In technical writing, naming a workflow is not enough; the reader also needs to know what each step produces.
- The phrase "reviewing papers and studying others research" would be stronger if you specified the goal more precisely. For example, are you extracting definitions, proof strategies, expository patterns, or open problems?
- Your note about collecting "patterns in mathematical writing" is promising. In future logs, try to record one or two concrete patterns you noticed, rather than only stating that you want to study them.
